We can't stop writing

Today I got on the train and wrote 136 characters. I then got to work and wrote somewhere around 10,000 characters. I then got back on the train and wrote 99 characters on Twitter and 151 characters on Facebook. I got in and scribbled a note on the sideboard – I don’t know how many characters that was. I then wrote a couple of emails and then I wrote this, so far it’s 303 characters…

Welcome to generation copy.

We never stop writing, and if it is it’s to start reading. We’re always on standby to share a pithy update with our friends or leave a witty response under somebody’s photo. If we’re lucky, we’ll be rewarded with a little thumbs up.

There’s already been a lot written about society’s increasing reliance on social media. “Is technology reducing pupils’ attention spans?” asks the media. For most 20-50 year olds in developed countries, Facebook, Twitter and Angry Birds are just a couple of touches away. And who can blame them for craving these distractions? If you told me ten years ago that I could access any piece of information I desired from my Nokia 3310 I think that I might have wet myself.

The media are probably correct: social media is probably decreasing attention spans and almost definitely encouraging a mindset of instant gratification. Nuts.

But, this social media thing as done some good: it has inspired a whole generation of habitual writers.

Writing (and being read) is no longer the preserve of the lucky few who mange to get in with a publisher or ad agency. Never has there been a period in history where the ability to write and to be read has been so easy.

I see genuinely witty, intelligent and funny snippets of copy on a daily basis. Today’s highlight was: “Mrs. Potato Head probably gives Mr. Potato head”. Okay, it’s not Shakespeare, but it features many of the attributes that a great copy does: humour, risk and brevity.

Social media has been blamed for frazzling attention spans, wasting time and promoting self indulgence, but what about the good stuff?

If used thoughtfully and sparingly it is a fantastic place to sharpen your skills and ability to produce short, to-the-point copy; something that takes lots of practice.
